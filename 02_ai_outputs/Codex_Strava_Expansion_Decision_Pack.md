# Codex Decision Pack: Strava Expansion Hypotheses

## 1. Executive Summary

My current recommendation is to prioritize **Strava Adventure / Active Travel** as the leading expansion hypothesis.

Why this wins:

- It has the strongest fit with Strava's existing route, community, map, and identity assets.
- It is already partially validated by competitor behavior, especially `komoot`, but Strava still has room to differentiate with social graph, clubs, and destination-driven discovery.
- It is highly prototype-friendly and presentation-friendly.
- It can open multiple monetization paths without immediately dragging the team into heavy medical, clinical, or employer-sales complexity.

My current top 3:

1. Strava Adventure / Active Travel
2. Strava Preventive Health / Active Longevity
3. Strava for Corporate Wellness

Biggest unresolved question:

Can the team show that **active travel** is a true market expansion, not just "better routes"? The pitch must clearly move from route utility into **destination planning, community experiences, and bookable active trips**.

## 2. Strava Summary

### What Strava is today

Based on Strava's official product and support pages, Strava is strongest as a **social fitness network** built around activity tracking, performance analysis, route discovery, competition, and community motivation. Its current product strengths include:

- activity tracking across many sports
- social feed and community identity
- routes and personalized route generation
- segments, clubs, and challenges
- training analysis and training plans
- live safety through Beacon

### Core strengths

The most important assets for an expansion strategy are:

- social graph and community
- large base of activity and route data
- map and exploration layer
- recurring engagement through goals, streaks, and competition
- credible premium subscription model
- strong active-lifestyle brand

### Monetization

Strava's official pricing page shows recurring subscription monetization, including monthly and annual plans, a family plan, and a bundled plan with Runna in the United States.

### Strategic implication

Inference:

Strava is already more than a tracker, but it is still under-monetized relative to the depth of its community, route intelligence, and identity graph. That creates room for expansion into adjacent markets where community + movement + place matter together.

### What competitors are telling us

Facts:

- `Nike Run Club`, `Runkeeper`, and `Garmin Connect` all lean hard into guided progression and structured training.
- `adidas Running` leans into rewards and event-linked engagement.
- `komoot` clearly proves demand for outdoor discovery, route planning, and multi-day adventure workflows.
- `COROS` and `Garmin` show that training infrastructure can anchor premium behavior even when social features are lighter.

Inference:

- A pure "better training" move is credible, but crowded.
- A pure "better routing" move is also crowded.
- A stronger angle is to take a validated competitor pattern and shift it into a more social, identity-rich, and community-native Strava product.

## 6. Top 3 With Tradeoffs

### 1. Strava Adventure / Active Travel

Why it made the top 3:

- strongest strategic fit with existing Strava map and community assets
- clearest competitor-informed expansion path because `komoot` proves planning demand
- most visually impressive prototype opportunity

Main upside:

- lets Strava enter travel and experiences without abandoning its identity

Main downside:

- if framed too narrowly, it can sound like a route feature instead of a market expansion

Biggest risk:

- the team under-defines the business model and turns the pitch into "Strava but better maps"

Why it is stronger than the other finalists:

- easier to prototype than preventive health
- less go-to-market friction than corporate wellness

### 2. Strava Preventive Health / Active Longevity

Why it made the top 3:

- the societal need is huge and well supported by public-health evidence
- Strava already has motivation, habit loops, and visible progress mechanisms

Main upside:

- large market and strong "why now" story

Main downside:

- brand fit is less obvious because Strava still feels performance-oriented to many users

Biggest risk:

- the team drifts into medical claims or vague wellness messaging

Why it is weaker than #1:

- harder to design a concrete and differentiated MVP without regulatory or positioning questions

### 3. Strava for Corporate Wellness

Why it made the top 3:

- clear monetization logic
- strong behavior-change mechanics through clubs and challenges
- supported by workplace physical-activity guidance and employee wellbeing trends

Main upside:

- easiest path to direct revenue beyond consumer subscriptions

Main downside:

- crowded B2B narrative and lower presentation wow

Biggest risk:

- becomes a generic HR tool instead of a distinct Strava-native product

Why it is weaker than #1:

- lower emotional pull and less compelling visual story for the jury

## 7. Final Recommendation

### Best hypothesis

**Strava Adventure / Active Travel**

### Why it wins

Facts:

- Strava already has routes, generated routes, maps, clubs, challenges, hiking/cycling support, and premium exploration features.
- `komoot` proves that outdoor planning, navigation, and multi-day adventure workflows have strong user demand.
- wellness travel and experience-led movement are growing segments, according to the `Global Wellness Institute`.

Inference:

This is the cleanest move from Strava's current base into a **new commercial arena**: active travel, destination discovery, and experience planning. It is neither a random adjacency nor a pure feature extension. It is a believable expansion powered by assets Strava already owns but does not fully monetize.

### Why the other finalists lost

- **Preventive Health / Active Longevity** lost because the market is huge but the first product wedge is less obvious and easier to make vague.
- **Corporate Wellness** lost because monetization is great but the product story is less distinctive and less visually memorable.

## 8. One-Page Thesis

### Hypothesis

Strava should expand into **active travel and outdoor experiences** by launching a destination-first product layer that helps users discover, plan, join, and book socially validated adventures.

### Why this market

Consumers increasingly spend on movement-linked experiences, not only on fitness tracking itself. Official `Global Wellness Institute` research points to continued growth in wellness travel, while `komoot` demonstrates strong demand for route-led outdoor discovery and navigation.

### Why now

Three forces line up:

- users increasingly want experiences, not only metrics
- Strava already has enough route and community data to power meaningful recommendations
- competitors validate planning demand, but no one owns the combination of **social graph + movement identity + destination discovery** as strongly as Strava could

### Why Strava

Strava's right to win comes from combining:

- route intelligence
- community proof
- local clubs and athlete identity
- premium subscribers already paying for exploration and analysis

Strava is not starting from zero. It is extending existing strengths into a larger travel-and-experiences use case.

### Target segment

First beachhead segment:

`25-45` year-old urban professionals who already run, cycle, or hike, travel several times per year, and want destination-aware active plans that feel social and easy to trust.

### Product concept

The product would shift Strava from "log your activity" to "plan your next active experience."

Core MVP:

- destination pages with curated route bundles
- multi-day active itineraries
- club and event overlays by destination
- community-validated highlights
- bookable local experiences or partner offers
- pre-trip save, in-trip navigation, post-trip social sharing

### Revenue model

Phase 1:

- premium feature bundle for destination planning and trip tools

Phase 2:

- affiliate or commission revenue from tours, rentals, guides, and stays

Phase 3:

- destination marketing partnerships and sponsored experience placements

### Wow factor for presentation

This idea is unusually demo-friendly:

- map-heavy
- lifestyle-heavy
- community-heavy
- easy to show in 3-5 screens

It also gives the team a story that feels bigger than a feature: **Strava becomes the operating system for active trips**.

### Key risks

1. The concept collapses into route planning instead of true travel expansion.
2. Monetization logic stays hand-wavy if partner economics are not specified.
3. The team underestimates how strong `komoot` already is in pure planning workflows.

### What to validate next

1. How large and attractive is the active travel / outdoor experience segment for Strava's likely audience?
2. Which monetization wedge is strongest first: premium planning, affiliate bookings, or destination partnerships?
3. Which user flow is the MVP anchor: destination discovery, trip planning, or joining local experiences?
4. How differentiated can the product be versus `komoot` and generic travel apps?

## 9. Suggested Workshop Questions

Use these in your team facilitation:

1. Which idea gives us the clearest answer to "why Strava, why now?"
2. Which idea is easiest to explain as a market expansion, not a feature?
3. Which idea gives us the strongest 3-5 screen prototype?
4. Which idea has the clearest first monetization wedge?
5. Which idea would survive aggressive jury questions about differentiation?
