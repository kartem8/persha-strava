# Strava Expansion Hypothesis Sprint

## Period

April 17-20, 2026

## Goal

By the end of April 20, 2026 the team should have:

1. A longlist of 10-15 expansion hypotheses for Strava
2. A shortlist of top 3 hypotheses
3. One selected hypothesis
4. A one-page thesis for the selected direction

## Context From The Assignment

The final project requires an expansion strategy for an existing IT product into an adjacent or completely new market. For Strava, the team must choose a new direction that is strategically plausible, large enough to matter, monetizable, visually prototypable, and compelling enough for a jury of product and business leaders.

The mentor's comment should be treated as a mandatory research direction:

"Have you reviewed Strava's direct competitors? There may already be interesting new functionality there."

The strongest early-stage selection criteria are:

1. Market size
2. Fit with Strava's core strengths
3. Monetization potential
4. Ease of prototyping
5. Wow-effect for the jury

## Working Principle

The goal of this sprint is not to find the "perfect" idea. The goal is to quickly filter many possible directions and select one hypothesis that can support the rest of the project:

- market validation
- competitor analysis
- product adaptation strategy
- AI-built prototype
- monetization and unit economics
- marketing funnel and creatives

## Team Roles For 6 People

1. Facilitator / Editor
   Owns the process, meeting flow, final decision log, and one-page thesis.
2. Strava Product Analyst
   Documents current product strengths, UVP, user flows, audience, monetization, and platform differences.
3. Market Scout A
   Searches for adjacent and new-market opportunities, trends, pain points, and demand signals.
4. Market Scout B
   Searches for competitors, substitutes, and expansion analogs.
5. Monetization Owner
   Evaluates business model logic and willingness-to-pay options for each hypothesis.
6. Prototype / Wow Owner
   Evaluates how easy each idea is to demonstrate in 3-5 prototype screens and how impressive it can look in the final presentation.

## Direct Competitor Scan

Before finalizing hypotheses, the team should explicitly review direct and close competitors of Strava.

This is important for two reasons:

1. Competitors may already have functionality that points toward high-potential expansion spaces.
2. Your final recommendation will be stronger if you can say whether the idea is:
   - already validated elsewhere
   - missing in Strava today
   - differentiated enough to matter

### Working Definition

There is no single official "direct competitor" list for Strava, so for this sprint treat these as the most relevant current direct or close-direct competitors because they overlap with Strava's core jobs-to-be-done: activity tracking, training support, route discovery, community motivation, and outdoor exploration.

### Recommended Competitor Set

#### Direct Competitors

1. Garmin Connect
   Device-centered training ecosystem with Garmin Coach plans, analytics, and strong athlete retention.
2. Nike Run Club
   Strong running-only competitor with guided runs, training plans, community motivation, and live location sharing.
3. adidas Running
   Multi-sport consumer fitness app with GPS tracking, challenges, leaderboards, training support, events, and rewards.
4. ASICS Runkeeper
   Running-focused app with personalized race training plans, guided workouts, goal support, and race discovery.
5. MapMyRun / MapMyFitness
   Longstanding tracking category player with route database, training plans, and workout tracking.

#### Close Or Partial Competitors

6. komoot
   Strong route planning and outdoor exploration competitor, especially relevant for hiking, cycling, and adventure use cases.
7. COROS ecosystem
   Strong training and coaching workflow for performance-oriented athletes, especially through training and coach tooling.

### Why These Competitors Matter

- Garmin Connect can reveal product patterns around coaching, planning, and device-led retention.
- Nike Run Club can reveal how coaching and motivation are packaged for runners.
- adidas Running can reveal how rewards and event participation drive engagement.
- Runkeeper can reveal how guided progression and race plans are framed.
- MapMyRun can reveal baseline category expectations around routes and training.
- komoot can reveal opportunities in outdoor exploration, route intelligence, and adventure planning.
- COROS can reveal coach-facing and performance-athlete workflows.

### Minimum Competitor Questions To Answer

For each competitor, collect answers to these questions:

1. What is the product's core job-to-be-done?
2. Which user segment does it serve best?
3. Which standout features overlap with Strava?
4. Which standout features go beyond Strava?
5. What monetization model does it use?
6. What expansion clues does it give us?
7. Which features feel transferable to a new Strava market?

### Competitor Scan Output

Create one simple table with:

- competitor
- target user
- standout features
- features Strava lacks or underplays
- monetization clues
- expansion clues for our project

### Suggested Current Feature Signals To Verify

Use current product pages and support pages to validate these:

- Strava: routes, segments, clubs, challenges, training plans, Beacon, analysis, group features
- Garmin Connect: Garmin Coach plans, training calendars, analytics, device ecosystem
- Nike Run Club: guided runs, training plans, weather prep, live run location sharing
- adidas Running: GPS tracking, challenges, leaderboards, adaptive plans, event check-ins, rewards
- Runkeeper: personalized race plans, guided workouts, race discovery
- komoot: route planning, sport-specific routing, multi-day routes, on-route weather
- COROS: training hub, coach workflows, athlete planning

### Interpretation Rule

Do not copy competitor features mechanically.

Instead ask:

- What user problem is the competitor solving?
- Why does that problem matter enough to shape a new market hypothesis?
- Can Strava solve it in a more social, data-rich, or community-driven way?

## Day-By-Day Plan

### April 17, 2026

#### Objective

Create a broad longlist of expansion hypotheses and align the team on criteria.

#### Steps

1. Run a 45-minute kickoff.
   Align on what counts as a true expansion, not just a small feature extension.

- Brief description for each

### April 18, 2026

#### Objective

Build a first-pass scoring model and eliminate weak hypotheses quickly.

#### Steps

1. Create the scoring matrix.
2. Validate whether top hypotheses are inspired by a real unmet gap versus something competitors already solved well.

   Only gather enough signal to know whether it deserves deeper work.

### Optional Internal Criteria

These do not need to be shown in the final deck, but they help the team decide better:

- Speed of validation
- Defensibility

## Red Flags

Reject or downgrade a hypothesis if:

- It is only a feature extension, not a real new market move
- There is no clear path to revenue
- Strava feels unnatural in the category
- The market story is hard to explain in one minute
- The prototype would be abstract or visually weak
- The team cannot see how to build a 10-minute pitch around it

## Facilitation Guide

### Rule 1

Do not debate personal taste. Debate evidence and scoring criteria.

### Rule 2

Do not research all 15 ideas equally deeply.

### Rule 3

Do not let the team choose an idea just because it is creative. It must also be strategically plausible and monetizable.

### Rule 4

If two ideas have similar business logic, prefer the one that will create a stronger demo and cleaner story for the jury.
